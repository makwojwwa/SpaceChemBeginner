In the previous section you have successfully input atoms, created a bond between them, and output the molecule. However, that particular solution does not apply all the available basic instructions. You can learn how to use other basic instructions and to do some more basic actions by modifying the solution you have created in Part 1. At the same time you will learn how to make your solution faster.

Rotating molecules, modifying instructions

  1. Open your solution, which should look like this:

  2. Click the Rotate instruction located under the reactor, drag it to the closest grid cell on the right side of the blue Bond+ instruction.

Notice that you added a red instruction to a blue path and the instruction lies outside the path. The reason is that the blue waldo is unable to execute red instructions and vice versa. However, this situation can be fixed.

  1. Right-click the Rotate instruction you have just placed.

  2. Click Blue Layer.

Now you can see that the color of the instruction switched to blue. The blue waldo can execute this instruction. You should check if something changed. To do this, click the Play button.

By default the Rotate instruction rotates your molecule counter-clockwise. This is why the molecule did not fit correctly in the output area and was not output, which led to a collision. Click Okay so that you can proceed to fix this situation:

  1. Right-click the Rotate instruction you have just placed.

  2. Click the symbol with an arrow rotating clockwise.

  3. Click the Play button.

The molecule fits the output area and the solution is correct. However, as you can see in the Performance tab, your solution uses 148 cycles now. This is the effect of the Rotate instruction, which is responsible for one additional cycle per loop. Fortunately, this situation can be fixed as well.

Making your solution faster

The simplest way to make your solution faster is to reduce the length of the path along which your waldos run. The shorter their paths, the faster they can return to the beginning of the algorithm. Let's find out if this rule can be applied to your solution.

  1. Click Continue...

    ... and then click the SpaceChem symbol shown in the image below to reopen the assignment.

It looks like both the red and blue loop can be reduced. Let's start with the blue loop. The atom will be input in the third row counted from the bottom of the reactor, while the Start instruction is placed in the second row counted from the bottom of the rector. You should change it.

  1. Click the blue Start instruction, drag it to the grid cell located directly above, and drop it there.

The loop fell apart and now you have to relocate other instructions to recreate it.

  1. Click the blue Arrow Up instruction located in the reactor, drag it to the grid cell with the blue Grab Drop instruction, and drop it there.

  2. Click the blue Input Î² instruction located in the reactor, drag it to the grid cell located immediately to the right of the Grab Drop instruction, and drop it there.

Notice that you have two blue and two red Grab Drop instructions. To avoid confusion, they should be modified:

  1. Right-click the left blue Grab Drop instruction and click the Grab symbol.

  2. Right-click the right blue Grab Drop instruction and click the Drop symbol.

  3. Right-click the left red Grab Drop instruction and click the Grab symbol.

  4. Right-click the right red Grab Drop instruction and click the Drop symbol.

Now the instructions are much more legible. And because you placed the Rotate instruction, you can drop the molecule earlier.

  1. Click the blue Drop instruction located in the reactor, drag it to the grid cell located directly above, and drop it there.

  2. Click the blue Output Ï‰ instruction located in the reactor, drag it to the grid cell located directly above, and drop it there.

  3. Click the blue Arrow Left instruction located in the reactor, drag it to the grid cell located directly above, and drop it there.

  4. Click the red Arrow Up instruction located in the reactor, drag it to the grid cell with the red Drop instruction, and drop it there.

  5. Click the red Arrow Left instruction located in the reactor, drag it to the grid cell with the red Start instruction, and drop it there.

The loops are shorter so now you should check your solution.

  1. Click the Play button.

Unfortunately, the reaction could not be completed and there is an error. After shortening the loops, both waldos are no longer synchronized â€“ the blue waldo reached the bonder faster than the red waldo and the bond was not added. This situation can be fixed as well.

Synchronizing the waldos

For a bond to be formed, both atoms must be present on adjacent bonders at the same time. You can try to synchronize the waldos in two ways. In this solution, the red waldo has to run along 8 grid cells to reach its bonder, while the blue waldo has to run only 7 grid cells to reach its bonder. So you should try moving the red waldo one cell closer to the bonder.

  1. Click Okay to return to the reactor view.

  2. Click the red Start instruction, drag it to the grid cell immediately to the left, and drop it there.

  3. Click the Play button.

Relocating the red Start instruction synchronized both waldos at the beginning, thanks to which a molecule was created and output. However, because the red loop is much shorter than the blue loop, the red waldo input and dropped its atom more often than the blue waldo was able to form a bond and output the molecule. The result was a collision.

You will fix this situation by adding Sync instructions.

  1. Click Okay to return to the reactor view.

  2. Click the Sync instruction located under the reactor, drag it to the grid cell immediately to the right the red Start instruction.

  3. Click the Tab toggler in the Layer Controls section to the right of the available instructions to switch to the blue layer.

  4. Click the Sync instruction located under the reactor, drag it to the grid cell immediately to the right of the blue Start instruction.

  5. Click the Play button.

Success! There were no collisions and your solution used 128 cycles.
