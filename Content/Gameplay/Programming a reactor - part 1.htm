Programming a reactor – part 1

Although each level needs to be solved in a different manner in terms of planning, the physical steps you are required to take are the same in terms of procedure. Regardless of the assignment, you always have to input atoms/molecules, grab them, change the direction of waldos, drop and output the required atom/molecule. Apart from that, you may need to bond atoms, remove bonds from molecules, rotate molecules, create branches, sense atoms and fuse atoms – depending on assignment.

The first, second and fourth assignment in the game are simple tutorials, which introduce a few basic instructions and some initial knowledge about reactions. This section summarizes everything you have learned during the initial four assignments and helps you solve the fifth assignment. Create an algorithm on your own or make use of the following procedure if you can't remember what to do next at a certain point. All steps are accompanied by screenshots, which will help you with the visual arrangement of instructions.

After opening any assignment you will see an empty reactor with the default starting setup: two Start instructions in the middle of the reactor grid. In this particular assignment, you will also see four bonders in the middle of the reactor grid, two input notes showing the atoms which will be input, and one output note showing the required molecule which must be output.

Inputting and grabbing atoms, redirecting waldos

  1. Click the Input ɑ instruction, drag it to a grid cell located along the red waldo path before the place where the atom will be input, and drop it there.

  2. Click the Grab Drop instruction, drag it to the grid cell where the atom will be input, and drop it there.

  3. Click the Arrow Down instruction, drag it to the grid cell where you have placed the Grab Drop instruction in the previous step, and drop it there.

Now you have to start creating an algorithm for the other atom. To do that, you should use the blue waldo.

  1. Click the Tab toggler in the Layer Controls section to the right of the available instructions.

    As a result, the available instructions will now be blue.

  2. Click the Input β instruction, drag it to a grid cell located along the red waldo path before the place where the atom will be input, and drop it there.

  3. Click the Grab Drop instruction, drag it to the grid cell where the atom will be input, and drop it there.

  4. Click the Arrow Up instruction, drag it to the grid cell where you have placed the Input β instruction in the previous step, and drop it there.

Adding bonds

Now your waldos will input both atoms and grab them. Next you have to create a bond between both atoms. You have two options: either one atom will be placed above the other or one atom will be placed next to the other. There is no difference between these options in terms of output - the orientation of the molecule doesn't matter. In this example, you will place one atom above the other.

  1. Click the Arrow Right instruction, drag it to the grid cell above the blue Grab Drop instruction, and drop it there.

  2. Click the Tab toggler in the Layer Controls section to the right of the available instructions to switch to the red layer.

  3. Click the Arrow Right instruction, drag it to the grid cell located two grid cells below the red Grab Drop instruction, and drop it there.

  4. Click the Grab Drop instruction, drag it to the grid cell with Bonder 1, and drop it there.

  5. Click the Tab toggler in the Layer Controls section to the right of the available instructions to switch to the blue layer.

  6. Click the Bond+ instruction, drag it to the grid cell with Bonder 3, and drop it there.

Now you have placed enough instructions to create a bond between the atoms. To find out if the algorithm is successful:

  1. Click the Play button and watch your partial solution.

Success! Both atoms were input and grabbed by the waldos and there was no collision. The red waldo dropped the atom on the bonder, while the blue waldo created a bond between the atoms, thanks to which the required molecule was created. Moreover, creation of the bond prevented the blue waldo from dropping the molecule. Now you can click the Stop button to stop your solution.

Outputting molecules

The blue waldo still holds the molecule and now it has to output it in the correct output area. The red waldo, in turn, has to return to the beginning of the algorithm.

  1. Click the Arrow Down instruction, drag it to the first grid cell in the Output ω area along the blue waldo path, and drop it there.

  2. Click the Grab Drop instruction, drag it to the grid cell below the Arrow Down instruction you have placed in the previous step, and drop it there.

  3. Click the Output ω instruction, drag it to the grid cell below the Grab Drop instruction you have placed in the previous step, and drop it there.

  4. Click the Arrow Left instruction, drag it to the grid cell where you have placed the Output ωinstruction in the previous step, and drop it there.

  5. Click the Tab toggler in the Layer Controls section to the right of the available instructions to switch to the red layer.

  6. Click the Arrow Up instruction, drag it to the first grid cell in the Output ψ area along the red waldo path, and drop it there.

  7. Click the Arrow Left instruction, drag it to the grid cell located two cells above the Arrow Up instruction you have placed in the previous step, and drop it there.

Theoretically your solution is complete. Feel free to check if that actually is the case. To do this:

  1. Click the Play button and watch your solution.

Success! The molecule was dropped in the correct output area and output, and both waldos returned to the beginning of the algorithm.
